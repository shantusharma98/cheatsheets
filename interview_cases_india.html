Swiggy 🇮🇳 India Retention

Order frequency on Swiggy has dropped 22% among users who ordered more than 5 times last month. Why, and how do you fix it?

HARD ▾
1

Clarify First

• Is this drop across all cities or Tier 1 only (Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru)?
• Did Zomato run a heavy discount campaign in this period?
• Was there a price change, delivery fee hike, or app update?
• Is it fewer orders per user or fewer active users overall?

2

MECE Structure — Why Users Stop Ordering

  • Value/Price: Delivery fees up, platform fee added (₹2–5/order), surge pricing perception
  • Product Experience: Late deliveries, wrong orders, app bugs post-update
  • Competition: Zomato Gold/Pro offer, Blinkit or Zepto groceries eating food occasions
  • Content/Discovery: Same restaurants repeating, no new curation, menu fatigue
  • External: WFH ended → office canteen back, seasonal (summer heat = less cooking out)
3

Key Metrics to Pull

Orders/User/Month Cart Abandonment Rate Avg Delivery Time Discount Redemption Rate App Rating (last 30 days) Reorder Rate
4

Recommendations

  • Quick win: "Order Again" nudge with 1-tap reorder of last meal + 10% off for power users
  • Loyalty fix: Swiggy One free trials for lapsed high-frequency users — subscription = stickiness
  • Discovery: "New near you" shelf featuring restaurants opened in last 30 days in user's 3km radius
  • Price optics: Show "You saved ₹X with Swiggy One this month" — make value visible
🇮🇳 India Context

Indian users are extremely price-sensitive. The ₹2 platform fee introduced in 2023 drove massive backlash. Swiggy One subscription at ₹299/month is the biggest retention lever — A/B test free 30-day trials on churning users.

Zomato 🇮🇳 India Growth

Zomato wants to grow its user base in Tier 2 cities (Jaipur, Indore, Coimbatore). How would you approach this?

MEDIUM ▾
1

Diagnose the Tier 2 Gap First

Why is Tier 2 underpenetrated? Ask: Is it supply-side (few restaurants listed)? Demand-side (low awareness/trust)? Or infrastructure (poor delivery partner density)?

2

Growth Framework — Supply × Demand × Infrastructure

  • Supply: Onboard local dhabas, cloud kitchens, regional chains (not just QSR) — Tier 2 users want local food, not Domino's
  • Demand: Hindi/regional language app UI, offline-to-online ads (auto-rickshaw wraps, college campus activations)
  • Trust: COD (Cash on Delivery) option — UPI penetration is lower in Tier 2, many still prefer cash
  • Pricing: ₹0 delivery fee on first 10 orders, lower minimum order value (₹99 vs ₹149)
  • Infrastructure: Hire local delivery partners aggressively — dark store model for faster coverage
3

Metrics to Track Success

New Users/Month (Tier 2) D30 Retention Rate Restaurant Density/km² Avg Delivery Time First Order Conversion %
🇮🇳 India Context

Tier 2 India has 400M+ users coming online. Swiggy and Zomato's next 100M users are here. Key insight: local language support + COD + lower price floor = the winning combo, not just copying the Tier 1 playbook.

BYJU'S 🇮🇳 India Retention

BYJU'S paid course completion rates are below 20%. Students are paying but not finishing. What's the problem and how do you fix it?

HARD ▾
1

Why It Matters

Low completion → no outcomes → no word-of-mouth → higher churn → higher CAC spiral. Completion rate is the North Star metric here.

2

Root Cause Tree

  • Motivation gap: Bought under sales pressure ("limited offer!") — no genuine intent to complete
  • Content quality: Long lectures, passive video consumption, no interactivity
  • Life friction: Class 10/12 students have board exams, tuition, school — not enough time
  • No accountability: Self-paced = easily ignored; no peer cohort, no live sessions
  • Technical: App performance issues on low-end Android phones (₹6,000–10,000 range)
3

Recommendations

  • Streak system: Daily learning streaks (like Duolingo) with parent-visible dashboard
  • Live cohort classes: Weekly live doubt-clearing sessions — creates commitment and community
  • Micro-content: Break 45-min lectures into 8-min modules — reduce activation energy
  • Parent engagement: Weekly SMS/WhatsApp report to parents ("Arjun completed 3 chapters this week") — social accountability
  • Outcome anchoring: Show "Students who complete this module score 18% higher in JEE mock tests"
🇮🇳 India Context

In India, parents are the real customer for EdTech. Engaging parents (WhatsApp reports, teacher calls) dramatically improves student completion. Also: app must run on Android Go devices — 60% of India uses budget phones.

Paytm 🇮🇳 India Metrics

Paytm's MAU is growing but transaction revenue per user is falling. How do you diagnose and address this?

HARD ▾
1

This is a Classic "Good Metric Up, Bad Metric Down" Trap

MAU growth is vanity if it brings low-value users. The real question is: are we acquiring the wrong users, or are existing users transacting less?

2

Decompose Revenue/User

Revenue/User = Transaction Frequency × Avg Transaction Value × Take Rate

Which of these three is falling?
• If frequency down → engagement problem (UPI competitors — GPay, PhonePe, CRED)
• If avg value down → new users are smaller spenders (Tier 3/rural users)
• If take rate down → pricing pressure from UPI mandate (0% MDR on UPI)

3

Solutions by Root Cause

  • If wrong user mix: Shift acquisition spend to high-value user segments (merchants, MSME owners, salaried urban)
  • If low engagement: Expand to financial services — Paytm insurance, loans, gold — where margins are 10–20x vs payments
  • If UPI pricing: Monetize through lending (BNPL), insurance distribution — payment is the entry point, not the revenue source
🇮🇳 India Context

UPI payments have zero MDR by government mandate — no fintech makes money on UPI transactions directly. The winning model is using UPI as a free customer acquisition channel, then monetizing through credit, insurance, and wealth products.

Ola / Uber India 🇮🇳 India Product

Driver cancellations on Ola are at 35% in metro cities, causing rider churn. How would you fix this as a Strategy Analyst?

HARD ▾
1

This is a Two-Sided Marketplace Problem

Any fix must balance driver incentives with rider experience. Penalizing drivers too hard increases driver churn → supply drops → worse for riders long-term.

2

Why Drivers Cancel — Root Causes

  • Low-value trip: ₹40 trip in peak traffic — driver loses money after fuel + commission
  • Long pickup: Rider is 3km away — driver prefers nearby rides
  • Destination mismatch: Dropping in an area with no return rides (airport-to-suburb)
  • Cash-only preference: Some riders pay cash, driver prefers digital
  • No-show fear: Rider books but doesn't show up — wastes 10 min
3

Recommended Fixes

  • Show destination upfront: Let drivers see drop-off before accepting — reduces post-accept cancels dramatically
  • Trip value guarantee: Guarantee ₹X minimum per hour for online drivers — removes low-value trip anxiety
  • Ride batching: Offer sequential trips — "Accept this ₹45 trip + guaranteed next trip in same area" = drivers see full value
  • Cancel penalty ladder: 3 cancels = 30-min timeout, 5 = 2-hour timeout — data shows this alone reduces cancels 18%
  • No-show rider penalty: Charge riders ₹20 if driver arrives and rider is absent after 3 min
🇮🇳 India Context

India rides are very short (avg ₹80–120) with tight margins. Drivers often prefer auto-rickshaw if trip value is low. The destination-upfront feature (Uber rolled this out in India in 2019) was a game-changer — directly addresses the #1 driver cancel reason.

Flipkart 🇮🇳 India Metrics

Flipkart's return rate has jumped to 28% for electronics. This is costing ₹200 Cr/month. What's driving this and how do you fix it?

MEDIUM ▾
1

Decompose Return Reasons

  • Product mismatch: "Not as described" — poor product images, vague specs, fake reviews
  • Defective/damaged: Supply chain handling damage or seller quality issues
  • Wardrobing/Fraud: Buyer uses product and returns (especially mobiles — IMEI switch)
  • Changed mind: Impulse buy during Big Billion Day sale, regret after
  • Counterfeit: Third-party marketplace sellers sending fake products
2

Segment Return Data

Pull returns by: Seller ID (is it concentrated in 20% of sellers = bad actor problem), Product category (mobiles vs laptops vs headphones), Return reason tag, Days-to-return (immediate = damaged/fraud, 7+ days = changed mind), User return history (are repeat-returners gaming the system?)

3

Fixes by Root Cause

  • Product accuracy: Mandate 360° video + verified spec sheets for all electronics sellers
  • Fraud prevention: IMEI verification on returns — if IMEI doesn't match, reject return
  • Seller accountability: Sellers with >15% return rate get suppressed in rankings, high-return sellers lose Flipkart Assured badge
  • Frictionless but not free: Show return cost to seller in their dashboard — creates accountability without hurting buyer trust
🇮🇳 India Context

Indian e-commerce has a unique "try and return" behavior driven by no-questions-asked return policies. IMEI-based mobile return fraud is a ₹500Cr+ annual problem for Flipkart/Amazon India. Balancing buyer trust vs. fraud prevention is the key tension in this case.

Meesho 🇮🇳 India Growth

You are a strategy analyst at Meesho. New seller onboarding has dropped 40% QoQ. How do you diagnose and fix this?

MEDIUM ▾
1

Funnel Analysis — Where Are Sellers Dropping Off?

Map the onboarding funnel: Awareness → Landing Page Visit → Registration Start → KYC Submission → First Product Listed → First Sale

Pull drop-off rate at each stage. A 40% drop could be at awareness (marketing stopped) or deep in funnel (KYC is too hard).

2

Root Causes by Stage

  • Awareness drop: Competitor (Amazon Easy Ship, Flipkart Seller Hub) ran acquisition campaigns
  • KYC friction: GST mandatory even for small sellers? Many Tier 2 sellers are unregistered
  • Trust: Word of mouth about delayed payments — check seller NPS and community forums
  • Economics: Commission structure changed — less attractive than before
3

Recommendations

  • Launch zero-commission first 30 days program for new sellers to reduce barrier to entry
  • Build vernacular onboarding — Hindi, Tamil, Bengali guided setup (Meesho's core market is non-English)
  • Simplify KYC — Aadhaar-only onboarding (no GST required for sellers below ₹40L/year threshold)
  • Create a Seller Success Score visible on dashboard — gamify progress to first sale
🇮🇳 India Context

Meesho's edge is reaching India's 15M+ small and micro sellers in Tier 2/3 towns. These sellers don't have GST, don't speak English, and distrust big platforms. Simplicity and trust — not features — is the growth lever here.

Amazon India 🇮🇳 India Product Strategy

Amazon Prime Video's watch time in India is 40% below global average despite high Prime subscriber count. Why and what would you do?

MEDIUM ▾
1

Hypothesis

Indian Prime users subscribed primarily for free delivery + Prime Day deals — video is a bonus, not the reason. Low content relevance = low video engagement despite high subscriber count.

2

Root Cause Segmentation

  • Content gap: Limited regional language content — Hindi content exists but Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, Marathi are thin
  • Competition: Hotstar has IPL (cricket) = the #1 watched content in India — Amazon has no sports rights
  • Discovery: Global recommendations don't surface Indian regional content effectively
  • Awareness: Many Prime subscribers don't even know video is included
3

Recommendations

  • Invest in regional language originals — one Tamil, one Telugu, one Bengali original/quarter
  • Add cricket highlights or local sports — even without full rights, match previews drive habit
  • Onboarding email campaign: "Did you know your Prime includes 5,000+ movies?" — convert delivery customers to video users
  • Surface "Top in your language" shelf on homepage based on device language setting
🇮🇳 India Context

India has 22 official languages. Any streaming platform ignoring regional content is fighting with one hand tied. Hotstar's dominance is entirely IPL cricket. Amazon's counter-move must be deep regional language originals + local cultural relevance.

Spotify India 🇮🇳 India Retention + Growth

Spotify's free-to-paid conversion in India is only 3% vs 27% globally. How do you improve it?

HARD ▾
1

Why India is Different — Context First

India has historically had free music (YouTube, Gaana, JioSaavn free tier). The mental model of paying for music doesn't exist for most users. This is a willingness-to-pay problem, not a product problem.

2

Conversion Funnel Leaks

  • Price: ₹119/month feels expensive vs JioSaavn free or YouTube free with ads
  • Perceived value gap: Free tier is too generous — users never feel the "pain" of being free
  • No must-have feature: No exclusive content or podcasts that force upgrade
  • Payment friction: Credit card penetration is low — UPI/debit-first India
3

Conversion Strategies

  • ₹7/day micro-pricing: Daily/weekly pass instead of monthly — removes commitment barrier
  • Tighten free tier: Limit skips to 3/hour, block offline mode, add mid-song ads — increase "pain"
  • Student plan at ₹59/month: Tap the 18–24 college segment with income and willingness to pay
  • Exclusive Bollywood early releases: New album 1 week early for Premium — creates urgency
  • UPI AutoPay: Enable seamless monthly subscription via UPI — removes payment friction
🇮🇳 India Context

Spotify launched ₹7/day and ₹13/week plans in India in 2019 — both outperformed the monthly plan in conversions. Micro-pricing is not just a discount; it's a different mental accounting. "₹7 for a day = a chai" vs "₹119/month = a real commitment."

Google Search 🌐 Global Metrics

Google Search's click-through rate (CTR) on organic results has dropped 15% YoY. Is this a problem? What do you do?

HARD ▾
1

Don't Assume It's Bad — Clarify First

A drop in organic CTR could mean: (a) more ads above the fold displacing organic, (b) featured snippets answering the query without a click (zero-click searches), or (c) users not finding what they need = bad. Only one of these is actually a problem.

2

Three Scenarios

  • Scenario A — Zero-click answers are up: Users got their answer directly on SERP (featured snippet, knowledge panel). Not a problem — this is Google's goal (best answer, fastest). Metric = query satisfaction rate
  • Scenario B — More paid ads: Google added a 4th ad slot. Organic pushed down. CTR down but revenue up. Business trade-off decision
  • Scenario C — Users are bouncing: Users are clicking but immediately hitting back = results are irrelevant. This is a real problem. Metric = time-on-result-page (Pogo-sticking rate)
3

Your Answer Should Show This Nuance

Don't panic at the metric drop. Investigate the cause first. If zero-click is up = improvement. If pogo-sticking is up = algorithm problem. The right North Star is Query Satisfaction Rate, not raw CTR.

LinkedIn 🌐 Global Growth

LinkedIn's feed engagement (likes, comments, shares) has dropped 18% among users with 500+ connections. Why and how do you fix it?

MEDIUM ▾
1

The Power User Paradox

Users with 500+ connections are LinkedIn's most valuable cohort (highest LTV, most content creators). Their disengagement signals a feed quality problem, not a user problem.

2

Root Cause Hypotheses

  • Feed is too noisy: 500+ connections = too many low-quality posts drowning signal
  • Engagement bait inflation: "Comment YES if you agree" posts devalued organic discussion
  • Content homogeneity: Algorithm amplifies the same viral post types — feels repetitive
  • Hustle-culture fatigue: Users tired of inspirational "I was rejected 50 times" posts
  • No close-network content: Feed shows viral strangers' posts, not updates from actual colleagues
3

Fixes

  • Relevance filter: Weight feed by "people you've interacted with in last 90 days" more heavily
  • Content type controls: Let users mute post types (polls, engagement bait) without unfollowing
  • Professional context signals: Show more posts from users in same industry/role — relevance over virality
  • Comment quality score: Rank "substantive" comments higher, suppress one-word reactions

Uber (Global) 🌐 Global Product

Uber Eats order volume is up 30% but profitability is down. How do you diagnose this as a strategy analyst?

HARD ▾
1

Revenue Up, Profit Down = Cost Problem

Profit = Revenue − Costs. If orders are up 30% but profit is down, either (a) revenue per order fell or (b) cost per order rose faster than order volume.

2

Decompose the P&L

  • Revenue side: Did discounts/promotions increase (lower net revenue per order)? Did we enter lower-ARPU markets?
  • Cost side: Driver incentive payouts up (driver shortage = surge bonuses)? Delivery distance longer (bad restaurant-to-customer matching)? Customer acquisition costs spiking?
  • Mix shift: New users order smaller orders + use coupons more → lower contribution margin
3

Solutions by Cause

  • If promo cost high: Cap discount spend, shift from blanket discounts to personalized targeted offers (ML-based)
  • If delivery cost high: Batch orders (deliver 2 orders in 1 trip) — reduces cost/order by ~25%
  • If driver incentives high: Forecast demand better → reduce surge events → reduce emergency incentive spend
  • If new user mix poor: Focus acquisition on high-LTV user segments, not lowest-CAC segments
